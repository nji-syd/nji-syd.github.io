An Introduction To The Boostrap For Quantitative Stock Selection

 Wednesday, 30 May 2018

Quantifying uncertainty is a fundamental and challenging task in financial modelling. In this post we’ll look at the bootstrap technique and how it can be applied to quantitative models. More generally, the bootstrap lies at the heart of a statistical technique called Bootstap Aggregation (bagging for short), which is the basis for machine learning algorithms such as random forest.

The Bootstrap

As an undergraduate I found statistics a grind, obscured by a deluge of mathematical formulas and jargon. But it needn’t be that way. The bootstrap is an empirical approach to inference which is both easy to use and free of many of the assumptions inherent in traditional parametric methods. The Bootstrap was developed by Bradley Efron in 1979. Here is an excerpt from Efron and Tibshirani’s excellent book, An Introduction to the Bootstrap

Statistics is the science of learning from experience, especially experience that arrives a little bit at a time. The earliest information science was statistics, originating in about 1650.

Most people are not natural-born staticians. Left to our own devices we are not very good at picking out patterns from a sea of noisy data. To put it another way, we are all too good at picking out non-existent patterns that happen to suit our purposes. Statistical theory attacks the problem from both ends. It provides optimal methods for finding a real signal in a noisy background, and also provides strict checks against the overinterpretation of random patterns

The explanations that we will give for the bootstrap, and other computer-based methods, involve explanations of traditional ideas in statistical inference. The basic ideas of statistics haven’t changed, but their implementation has. The modern computer lets us apply these ideas flexibly, quickly, easily and with a minimum of mathematical assumptions.

We can use the bootstrap to explore the uncertainty around a specific model, or compare the relative uncertainty of two or more models. As with any kind of financial risk analysis, the results are only as good as the assumptions on which they are based. If the data generating process is non-stationary, the results will be unreliable.

If we think about the historical performance of a stock portfolio, we have a empirical distribution of returns. We might want to infer metrics from the data, perhaps the annualized return, Sharpe ratio, or maximum drawdown of the portfolio. Statistically speaking, the true value of these quantities are unknown. If that sounds a little abstract, the example below will hopefully make things clearer. Then we will look to how the bootstrap allows for the estimation of confidence levels in the observations we have.

Dice Example

Suppose we roll a dice 12 times. The dice has 6 sides so we know the chance of any outcome is 1/6. But suppose we didn’t know this, and we want to estimate the true probability from the empirical data. Suppose we roll:

5,4,5,3,3,6,4,5,4,1,3,6

The true and empirical distribution can be summarized as follows:

Table 1: True and Observed Dice Probabilities

Value Rolled 1 2 3 4 5 6
True Probability 2/12 2/12 2/12 2/12 2/12 2/12
Observed Probability 1/12 0/12 3/12 3/12 3/12 2/12

Let’s denote the true probability distribution \(F\) and the observed distribution \(F^*\)

We know the true mean \(\mu\) of the distribution of dice rolls \(F\) is equal to:

\(=x_1*p(x_1) +\) \(x_2*p(x_2) +\) \(x_3*p(x_3) +\) \(x_4*p(x_4) +\) \(x_5*p(x_5) +\) \(x_6*p(x_6)\)

\(=1 * 1/6 +\) \(2* 1/6 +\) \(3* 1/6 +\) \(4* 1/6 +\) \(5* 1/6 +\) \(6* 1/6\)

\(= (1+2+3+4+5+6)/6\)

\(= 3.5\)

Where p is the true probability for \(F\). But we are pretending we don’t know the true distribution, rather all we know is the empiricle distribution \(F^*\). The empirical mean \(\overline{x}\) from Table 1 is:

\(=x_1*p(x_1) +\) \(x_2*p(x_2) +\) \(x_3*p(x_3) +\) \(x_4*p(x_4) +\) \(x_5*p(x_5) +\) \(x_6*p(x_6)\)

\(=1 * 1/12 +\) \(2* 0 +\) \(3* 3/12 +\) \(4* 3/12 +\) \(5* 3/12 +\) \(6* 1/6\)

\(= 4.08\)

Which is just the mean of sample:

\(=\dfrac{5,4,5,3,3,6,4,5,4,1,3,6}{12}\)

\(= 4.08\)

The bootstrap allows us to asses confidence in the sample mean of 4.08. Let’s see how.

The Bootstrap Process

We start by sampling the observed data \(F^*\) with replacement \(n\) times, where \(n\) is the size of the sample (in this case 12 because the dice was rolled 12 times). Let’s denote the observed sample as \(x\), the first bootstrap sample as \(x{^*_1}\), and the mean of the first bootstrap sample \(\overline{x{^*_1}}\)

\(x\): 5,4,5,3,3,6,4,5,4,1,3,6

\(x{^*_1}\): 5,5,3,3,4,6,3,4,3,1,6,4

\(\overline{x{^*_1}}\) \(=\) \(3.92\)

Now we repeat the sampling process many times. To keep things simple, we’ll resample just 20 times. In practice we might choose 1000x.

\(x{^*_1}\): 5,5,3,3,4,6,3,4,3,1,6,4 \(x{^*_2}\): 3,4,3,3,3,3,6,3,1,5,5,3 \(x{^*_3}\): 4,3,1,4,5,3,5,4,3,5,6,3
\(x{^*_4}\): 5,6,6,4,1,4,5,4,4,4,5,5 \(x{^*_5}\): 4,5,4,4,5,5,3,5,5,6,5,4 \(x{^*_6}\): 4,4,4,3,6,6,5,5,5,6,5,4
\(x{^*_7}\): 5,6,6,5,3,3,6,3,3,5,3,5 \(x{^*_8}\): 5,3,4,5,6,3,6,5,3,5,6,5 \(x{^*_9}\): 6,1,6,3,6,4,3,5,3,3,5,3
\(x{^*_{10}}\): 4,5,6,5,5,5,3,3,5,4,6,5 \(x{^*_{11}}\): 4,6,4,4,5,5,3,4,3,3,4,4 \(x{^*_{12}}\): 5,4,5,3,4,4,5,1,5,3,5,6
\(x{^*_{13}}\): 3,6,4,4,6,5,4,5,6,5,6,4 \(x{^*_{14}}\): 5,6,6,3,3,4,1,3,4,6,3,4 \(x{^*_{15}}\): 6,1,1,3,5,3,6,3,5,1,4,5
\(x{^*_{16}}\): 3,3,5,3,5,6,1,3,1,5,5,6 \(x{^*_{17}}\): 6,1,6,4,6,3,6,5,6,3,5,3 \(x{^*_{18}}\): 1,3,3,3,5,3,3,3,6,4,6,4
\(x{^*_{19}}\): 4,4,6,6,5,5,3,4,1,5,3,5 \(x{^*_{20}}\): 4,4,3,5,4,5,3,5,4,4,3,5

And here is where it gets interesting. Any statistic \(u\) we calculate on \(x\) (in this case the mean), we can also calculate on \(x^*\). It turns out the empirical bootstrap is able to approximate the variation in \(u\) via the variation in \(u^*\). In this case \(u\) is the mean, but it might also be the variance or Sharpe ratio. To do this we calculate:

\(\delta\) \(=\) \(\overline{x^*}\) \(-\) \(\overline{x}\)

For the first sample we have:

\(\delta^*_1\) \(=\) \(3.92\) \(-\) \(4.08\)

\(\space\space\thinspace\thinspace\) \(=\) \(-0.16\)

For the 20 resamples above we have:

\(\delta^*\) \(=\)-0.16 -0.58 -0.25 0.34 0.50 0.67 0.34 0.59 -0.08 0.59 0.00 0.09 0.75 -0.08 -0.50 -0.25 0.42 -0.41 0.17 0.00

The deltas \(\delta^*\) provide a non parametric estimate of the distribution of the observed sample statistic \(\overline{x}\) around the true statistic \(\mu\), even though we don’t know the true value of \(\mu\)\(^{1}\). We have 20 values, so each value approximates 5% of the probability distribution. We estimate the 90% confidence interval by adding the 1st and 20th ordered values to the sample mean.

[ 4.08-.58 < \(x\) < 4.08+.75 ]

\(\space\space\space\space\space\)[ 3.50 < \(x\) < 4.83 ]

In this case the true value 3.5, lies on the lower confidence interval. Here we used only 20 resamples to keep it simple, but to get good confidence estimates we would have used many more resamples.

Finally it’s worth noting that the bootstrap didn’t improve upon the sample estimate of 4.08 for the mean of the dice, it only helped estimate the uncertainty of it. You’ll also recall that we only rolled the dice 12 times. This is the root cause of our rather poor estimate \(\overline{x}=\) 4.08 and considerable uncertainty. Increasing the sample size of 12 will improve the estimate \(u\) \(=\) \(\overline{x}\). In doing so, the bootstrap sample size will also be larger resulting in tighter confidence intervals.

A Financial Model

Suppose we have a model for scoring stocks. We’ve backtested the model by applying it to some universe of stocks over some historical time frame. Specifically, each month we sort all stocks in the universe into 10 equal sized portfolios according to our scoring model, and then track the returns for the next month.

Bootstraping Timeseries

Timeseries problems introduce an additional challenge due to the potential for autocorrelation. The standard bootstrap assumes the ordering of the samples makes no difference, that is the samples are independent. However we can use a technique known as the block bootstrap to handle autocorrelation. There are a number of variants to the block bootstrap\(^3\), but all are based on the idea of capturing dependency structure in the timeseries by sampling chunks or blocks of the observed sample at a time.

One methodology which we’ll adopt here is know as the Circular Block Bootstrap (CBB). CBB creates blocks of consecutive data point equal to the block size \(b\), wrapping the samples at the end of the timeseries with those at the beginning. For example, consider a toy sample of 6 data points (A -> F):

A, B, C, D, E, F

CBB samples are shown below for a block size of 3. Rather than sampling 6 points independently from the original sample, the CBB samples 2 blocks independently.

A,B,C B,C,D C,D,E D,E,F E,F,A F,A,B

OK. Now lets look at the distribution of Sharpe ratios for the QVG model over the 13 year period. Below we use 1000 bootstrap samples with a block length of 12 (returns are monthly so the block length is 12 months). The observed Sharpe ratio and 90% confidence intervals are shown.

Figure Three - Distribution of Sharpe Ratio 2000-2012 (In Sample)

For the Sharpe ratio the 90% intervals are [0.14, 1.2]. The distribution is clearly skewed to the right, where the tail is longer. The centre line at 0.58 is the sample Sharpe ratio. Say we’d like to identify confidence intervals for the following 2 year period, can we use the intervals found above?

The answer is no, because we can be less sure about a two year horizon than we can about a 13 year horizon, due to smaller sample size. Figure Four shows the same CBB simulation as above but with a sample size of 2 years, rather than 13.

Figure Four - Distribution of Sharpe Ratio 2000-2012, with Two Year Horizon

Wow! Glad we didn’t assume a normal distribution. The smaller sample size has expanded the 5% and 95% confidence intervals from [0.14, 1.2] to [-0.26, 2.3].

The out of sample model performance for the subsequent 2 year period Jan 2013 to Jan 2015 is shown below.

Figure Four - Annualized Decile Returns - QVG Model (Out of Sample)

Figure Five - Top Decile Cumulative Return (Out-of-Sample)

The realized Sharpe ratio was 2.33. So the bootstrap would suggest the out-of-sample result is unusually good, but to be expected around 5% of the time.

As discussed earlier, the confidence interval values are only reliable to the extent that the insample period is representative of the out of sample period. In this case the GFC period represents around 12% of insample data, which is probably driving overly pessimistic estimates.

Quantifying Uncertainty

Uncertainty is sometimes broken down into aleatory and epistemic components. Aleatory refers to sources of variability that are purely random, whereas epistemic refers to limitations of the model and available data to capture the dynamics of the underlying system.

In the context of the dice example, the aleatory uncertainty comes from the multiple facets of the dice. No matter how many times we observe the dice, there is always random variation which we quantify in terms of the probability of each outcome. The epistemic uncertainty is characterized by alternate possible probability distributions\(^4\).

Specifically, we used the bootstrap to estimate the epistemic uncertainty of dice probabilities. Because we only had 12 samples the epistemic uncertainty was high. As the dice is rolled more times, our estimate of the aleatory variability improves and the epistemic uncertainty decreases towards zero.

In the dice experiment, epistemic uncertainty was limited to sampling error, but in the world of quantitative finance epistemic uncertainty includes limitations of the model itself and non-stationarity. We’ve seen that the bootstrap can estimate some classes of epistemic uncertainty such as sampling error, but other sources are out of reach. In the context of the financial model sources of non-stationarity might include:

  • Changes in the accounting standards upon which the model is based
  • Ad hoc crises (eg Global Financial Crisis)
  • Increased competition from other investors implementing similar strategies

Finally we can think of epistemic uncertainty in terms of the great quote by J.M Keynes\(^5\)

The sense in which I am using the term [uncertainty] is that in which the prospect of a European war is uncertain, or the price of copper and the rate of interest twenty years hence, or the obsolescence of a new invention, or the position of private wealth-owners in the social system in 1970. About these matters there is no scientific basis on which to form any calculable probability whatever. We simply do not know.

Summary

In this post I’ve endeavoured to provide an introduction to the technique of bootstrapping, as well as convey some of the opportunities and pitfalls of its application to quant finance.
